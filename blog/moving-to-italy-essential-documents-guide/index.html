1. Introduction: Why Italy and Why Documents Matter

Italy has become one of Europe's most sought-after destinations for expats, attracting over 500,000 foreign residents. Whether you're drawn to the artistic heritage of Florence, the canals of Venice, the culinary traditions of Bologna, or the Mediterranean coast of the Amalfi Peninsula, moving to Italy is an adventure that promises cultural richness, historic beauty, and a lifestyle envied worldwide. The country's economic resilience, strong regional identities, and deep-rooted family values continue to appeal to remote workers, retirees, entrepreneurs, and families seeking a fresh start in Southern Europe.

Yet behind the romance of Italian life lies a complex bureaucratic system that has intimidated countless expats. Italy's administrative framework—often criticized for its labyrinthine regulations—requires careful attention to detail and proper documentation from day one. The Italian government's approach to administrative procedures reflects a long historical tradition of regulation and formality, where every step is recorded, verified, and cross-referenced across multiple agencies. Getting your paperwork right at the outset isn't just a formality; it's the foundation for legal residency, access to healthcare, employment rights, tax compliance, and peace of mind throughout your stay.

The stakes are significant. Arrive in Italy without the correct documents, and you risk visa overstays, difficulties accessing public services, complications with employment and employer compliance, and potential fines that can reach several hundred euros. The Italian government has become stricter about documentation requirements in recent years, particularly following EU immigration reforms and heightened security screening. A single missing apostille or untranslated certificate can delay your residency application by weeks or even months. Banks may refuse to open accounts. Employers may be unable to hire you. Healthcare providers may question your eligibility. These aren't hypothetical scenarios—they happen regularly to unprepared expats.

This comprehensive guide walks you through every essential document you'll need—from before you leave home through your first months in Italy. We cover the Codice Fiscale (tax ID number that serves as your unique identifier), Permesso di Soggiorno (residence permit with specific requirements for different visa categories), AIRE registration for returning Italians, municipal registration processes, banking, healthcare, property rental, and business setup. We provide real office names, realistic timelines based on current processing backlogs, actual costs including administrative fees, and the exact steps Italian bureaucracy requires. We also explain the why behind each requirement, helping you understand not just what to do but how the Italian system works.

Why does this matter? Because Italian bureaucracy, while demanding, is entirely navigable when you know the rules. The system isn't designed to be difficult—it's designed to be thorough, comprehensive, and verifiable. Once you understand the logic, you can work with it rather than against it. By the end of this guide, you'll understand not just what documents you need, but why each one matters, where to get them, what to expect at each step, and how to prepare for potential complications. You'll move to Italy with confidence, knowing you've done it right.

2. Before You Leave Home: Pre-Departure Essentials

Understanding Apostilles and Certified Translations

The single most important concept for moving to Italy is the apostille—and this cannot be overstated. An apostille is an official certification that authenticates the origin of a public document. Established by the Hague Convention of 1961, an apostille certifies that a document issued by one signatory country is legitimate for use in another signatory country without additional authentication. Most countries, including the United States, UK, Canada, and Australia, are signatories to this convention. This means your documents, once apostilled, are recognized across all signatory nations, including Italy.

Think of an apostille as a special official seal or certificate of authenticity that says, "This document is genuine and comes from an authorized source in our jurisdiction." Without it, Italian authorities will not accept your documents as legitimate, regardless of how official they appear. This applies across the board: birth certificates, marriage certificates, death certificates, divorce decrees, educational credentials, employment history, professional certifications, and any other official document you'll need for Italian residency, employment, or legal purposes.

Getting an apostille is straightforward in concept but requires knowing the correct authority. In the United States, apostilles are issued by the Secretary of State's office in the state where the document was issued (usually your state of birth for birth certificates, or the state where your marriage was registered). In the UK, they're obtained from the Foreign Office Apostille Section or through certain county courts. In Canada, each province handles its own apostilles through the provincial government. In Australia, state governments issue apostilles. The process is straightforward: contact the relevant office, pay a fee (typically $10–30 per apostille), submit the original document or a certified copy, and receive the apostille either immediately (if in-person) or within 1–4 weeks (if by mail), with expedited services available in many jurisdictions.

Italy requires certified translations of most documents, and this is not a step you can shortcut. Using Google Translate or having a bilingual friend translate won't work—Italian law requires translations by a certified translator (traduttore certificato), usually an avvocato (lawyer) trained in legal translation or a professional translator registered with the court system (albo dei traduttori). A certified translation includes the translator's official stamp, signature, professional registration number, and a statutory declaration of accuracy. The translator certifies that the translation accurately reflects the original document and is fit for official use. Costs range from €50–150 per page, depending on the complexity of the language, technical terminology, and the translator's experience. Technical documents like diplomas, contracts, or medical records may cost more than standard translations.

The timeline matters critically: you should obtain apostilles and translations before leaving home. Many document services can be handled remotely via mail or online portal, but it's far easier to do this before the move when you have time, resources, and access to original documents. Once you're in Italy, obtaining apostilles from your home country becomes complicated—you'll need to mail requests internationally, coordinate with government offices across time zones, and wait for postal delivery. Plan to complete this process 2–3 months before your intended move date, allowing time for processing delays and unexpected requests for additional documentation.

Essential Documents to Gather and Certify

Passport: This is non-negotiable and must be your very first priority. Your passport must be valid for at least three months beyond your intended stay in Italy. If yours is expiring soon, renew it before moving. You'll need your passport for the Permesso di Soggiorno application, bank account opening, healthcare registration, and countless administrative processes. Bring multiple passport copies (5–10) to Italy; you'll use them repeatedly, and while you can get additional copies made in Italy, it's easier to have them from home. Keep your original passport safe in a secure location—losing it in Italy requires replacement through your embassy and is expensive and time-consuming.

Birth Certificate: Italy requires the original or a certified copy. Obtain this from the vital records office in your state or country of birth. Get an apostille from the same office (they're often in the same location). Have it translated into Italian by a certified translator—this is mandatory. Keep multiple certified copies; you'll use this document for residency registration, Codice Fiscale application, healthcare enrollment, and possibly other legal matters. Some offices may request a "long-form" birth certificate that includes parental information; verify which format you need before ordering.

Marriage Certificate (if applicable): If you're married, bring the original marriage certificate with apostille and certified Italian translation. If you're divorced or widowed, bring the divorce decree (apostilled and translated) or death certificate (apostilled and translated). Any change in marital status must be documented with official certificates. These are essential for spousal residency applications, joint banking, property ownership, and inheritance matters.

Proof of Income or Employment: For visa and residency purposes, Italy requires evidence of financial stability and ability to support yourself. This might be a formal letter from your employer on company letterhead, your last two years of income tax returns (apostilled and translated), or bank statements showing regular deposits consistent with employment or business income. If you're self-employed or retiring, bring profit and loss statements, business registration documents, or proof of pension income (often a letter from your pension provider). These documents don't always need apostilles, but they should be certified or official documents from recognized institutions. If your income is irregular or comes from multiple sources, prepare a comprehensive explanation along with supporting documents showing stability.

Proof of Accommodation: You'll need to show where you'll be living in Italy—this is a fundamental requirement for residency registration. This can be a rental agreement (contratto d'affitto), a letter from a landlord confirming they're renting to you, a property purchase agreement if you're buying, or even a notarized letter from an Italian friend or family member confirming they're housing you temporarily. The accommodation document should include the complete address (street, number, city, postal code), the owner's or landlord's full name, their contact information, signature, and date. Ensure the document is clear, specific, and dated appropriately relative to your intended move date.

Health Insurance Documentation: If you're bringing private health insurance, have copies of your policy document or a letter from your insurer confirming coverage in Italy and the geographical areas covered. For EU citizens, the EHIC (European Health Insurance Card) or its successor EDHIC is crucial—request it from your home country's health system before leaving. This card provides emergency and necessary medical care coverage throughout the EU. Non-EU citizens must show they have health insurance; without it, your residency application may be rejected or conditioned on obtaining coverage.

Educational Credentials (if relevant): If you plan to work, study, or practice a profession in Italy, bring original diplomas, degrees, or transcripts with apostilles and certified translations. Educational credentials are required for professional registration, university enrollment, or employment in regulated fields. If you hold professional credentials or licenses (engineering, law, medicine, accounting), bring those documents apostilled and translated as well.

Police Clearance Certificate: Italy may request a criminal records check from your country of origin, particularly if you're applying for a long-term residence permit, working in certain sectors, or if the authorities conduct background checks. This is called a "certificato del casellario giudiziale" equivalent from your home country. It must be recent (usually within 3–6 months of application) and apostilled. Order this well in advance through your local police force, FBI (in the US), national police records office, or equivalent authority—it can take 4–8 weeks to obtain and may require in-person visits to a police station or courthouse to request.

Codice Fiscale Pre-Registration (For Italian Citizens and Eligible Expats)

Here's an often-overlooked advantage that can save you significant time and administrative effort: if you have Italian heritage or are an Italian citizen by descent, or if you're an Italian citizen returning home, you can sometimes pre-register for a Codice Fiscale before arriving in Italy. This tax identification number is essential for every legal and administrative process in Italy, and normally it can only be obtained after arrival in person. However, Italian consulates in certain countries can issue it to eligible persons before departure, which means you arrive already holding this critical document.

If you're returning to Italy as an Italian citizen or claiming Italian citizenship by descent, contact your nearest Italian consulate before your move to ask if they can issue a Codice Fiscale. You'll need to provide proof of Italian citizenship (or eligibility for citizenship by descent) along with the standard documents (passport, birth certificate with apostille, etc.). Even if the consulate can't issue it directly, they can often guide you through remote registration with the Agenzia delle Entrate (tax authority) or connect you with designated local offices that can process it before your arrival.

For non-Italian citizens, the Codice Fiscale can only be obtained in Italy from the Agenzia delle Entrate, but you can often begin the application process before arrival through certain online channels, or by having a representative file preliminary paperwork on your behalf through an accountant or lawyer.

AIRE Registration for Italians Returning Home

If you're an Italian citizen who has lived abroad and registered with AIRE (Anagrafe degli Italiani Residenti all'Estero—Registry of Italians Resident Abroad), you must understand the de-registration process before returning. AIRE is a mandatory registry for Italian citizens living outside Italy; it records your residence abroad and affects your tax status, voting rights, and civic obligations. When you return to Italy permanently, you must de-register from AIRE and register in the Italian municipal registry (Anagrafe). Failure to do this creates legal complications and can affect your tax compliance and access to services.

Before returning, contact the Italian consulate in your current country of residence to begin the AIRE de-registration process. Most consulates allow this by mail or through online consulate portals—you'll need to submit a request with your personal details, AIRE registration information, and intended return date to Italy. Once you arrive in Italy and complete municipal registration at your local Comune (described in detail below), the municipality will automatically notify AIRE of your return and complete the de-registration process, removing you from the AIRE registry and confirming your residence in Italy.

3. Italian Residency and Registration Process

Step 1: Permesso di Soggiorno (Residence Permit)

The Permesso di Soggiorno is your legal residence permit and the foundation of your legal status in Italy. Even if you're an EU citizen with freedom of movement within the EU, you'll need to register your residence with Italian authorities. Non-EU citizens must obtain a Permesso di Soggiorno; EU citizens must register their residence. Both processes are legally mandatory and must be completed within specific timeframes of arrival—typically within 8 days for non-EU citizens and within 3 months for EU citizens.

For EU/EEA Citizens (Including UK since 2020): EU citizens should register their residence with the local Questura (police headquarters) or municipal office within three months of arrival. No visa is required for EU citizens to live in Italy under freedom of movement provisions, but registration is compulsory. You'll need your passport, proof of accommodation (rental agreement or letter from landlord), proof of employment or income (employment contract, payslips, or proof of self-employment), and health insurance documentation. Registration is usually free and takes 1–2 weeks. The residence certificate you receive is essential for subsequent registrations and applications. Some municipalities have integrated the EU registration into their municipal processes; check your local Comune website for procedures.

For Non-EU Citizens: You should ideally arrange a residence permit (Permesso di Soggiorno) before arriving in Italy, though this is not always possible depending on your visa category. If you haven't obtained one before arrival, you must apply at the local Questura within 8 days of entry. The Questura is the provincial police headquarters and is the sole authority that issues residence permits. Every Italian province has a Questura; find yours through www.interno.gov.it. You'll need: your passport (original and copies), completed application form (Modello per istanza di permesso di soggiorno—available at the Questura or www.interno.gov.it), four passport-sized photos (4 x 4 cm, identical), proof of accommodation with full address and landlord details, proof of financial means (bank statements, employment contract, or income documentation), travel insurance or health insurance coverage, self-addressed envelope with return postage, and the appropriate application fee or stamp duty.

The type of Permesso di Soggiorno you need depends on your reason for being in Italy: lavoro (employment), lavoro autonomo (self-employment), soggiorno (general residence), studio (student), riunificazione familiare (family reunification), pensionato (retirement), or other specific categories. Processing time is typically 30–60 days from submission, though many Questuras in major cities have severe backlogs. Rome's Questura often has waiting times of 2–3 months. Milan and Naples also experience significant delays. Smaller cities and regional towns often process faster. Many Questuras now require appointments booked online through the "Portale Unico dell'Immigrazione" (Unified Immigration Portal) at www.immigrazione.it. You must create an account, select your local Questura, choose a service type, and book an available appointment—this can take weeks, so begin immediately upon deciding to move.

The Permesso di Soggiorno is issued as a sticker in your passport (though this is gradually being phased out) or, increasingly, as a separate physical card or digital certificate, valid for 2–5 years depending on the permit type. When it expires, you must renew it at the same Questura. Renewals also require appointments through the online portal and take 30–60 days. Start the renewal process at least 60 days before expiration to avoid any gap in legal status.

Cost: Non-EU citizens pay a diritto di marca (stamp duty) of approximately €30–50 for the permit itself, plus postage fees if applying by mail. No official government fee exists beyond the stamp duty, but some Questuras may request a "contribution" or administrative charge; €100–200 is common though not technically mandatory. Budget conservatively and expect to pay at least €150–200 total for the application and associated costs.

Step 2: Iscrizione Anagrafica (Municipal Registry Registration)

Once you have your Permesso di Soggiorno or (as an EU citizen) once you've registered residence with the police, you must register with your local Comune (municipality). This is called iscrizione anagrafica and is mandatory within 20 days of establishing residence in Italy. This is not optional—it's a legal requirement, and failure to register can result in administrative fines and complications with other services. The Anagrafe (Registry Office) is typically part of the municipal government and is often located in the town hall (palazzo del Comune or municipio).

Visit the Anagrafe office of your municipality with the following documents: your original passport or copy certified by a notary, your Permesso di Soggiorno (or residence registration certificate for EU citizens), proof of accommodation such as a rental agreement (contratto d'affitto) showing your name and the address, or a letter from your landlord on their letterhead confirming you're renting the property and the address, and a completed "Istanza per l'Iscrizione all'Anagrafe" form (available at the municipality office, or downloadable from the municipality website). Some municipalities now accept online applications through their websites—check your specific municipality's portal (e.g., comune.roma.it, comune.milano.it, etc.).

When you arrive at the Anagrafe, inform staff that you're a new resident applying for registration. They may ask clarifying questions about your residency, employment status, and accommodation. Processing time is typically 1–3 weeks from submission. Once registered, you'll receive a "Certificato di Residenza" (residency certificate)—this is absolutely crucial. You'll need this document repeatedly for opening bank accounts, enrolling in healthcare, applying for Italian documents, and countless other administrative processes. Request 5–10 copies as they're inexpensive (€0.50–2 each) and you'll use them frequently.

Cost: Registration itself is free, though some municipalities charge a small administrative fee or stamp duty (€5–15). Requesting additional copies of your residency certificate costs approximately €0.50–2 per copy depending on the municipality.

Step 3: Codice Fiscale (Tax Identification Number)

The Codice Fiscale is a 16-character alphanumeric code assigned by the Italian Tax Agency (Agenzia delle Entrate) to every resident and anyone earning income in Italy. It's the primary identifier for every Italian resident and functions as your tax ID, social security number, insurance identifier, and the key to accessing virtually every service in Italy. You cannot open a bank account, sign an employment contract, enroll in healthcare, rent property, or conduct most legal business without it. Its importance cannot be overstated—your Codice Fiscale is as essential as your passport.

To obtain your Codice Fiscale, visit the local office of the Agenzia delle Entrate (tax authority) with your original passport (or certified copy), your residency certificate from the Anagrafe (Certificato di Residenza), and certified Italian translations of your birth certificate and marriage certificate (if applicable). You'll also need to complete and submit form AA4/8 (Richiesta del Codice Fiscale per Cittadini dell'Unione Europea—for EU citizens) or form AA4/4 (for non-EU citizens). These forms are available online at www.agenziaentrate.gov.it or at the office itself.

Processing is typically immediate to within 48 hours. You'll receive either a paper certificate (Certificato di Attribuzione del Codice Fiscale) or a digital confirmation that you can print, both showing your assigned Codice Fiscale. The Agenzia delle Entrate has offices in every Italian city, often with multiple locations in major urban areas and neighborhoods. Many now offer online booking through www.agenziaentrate.gov.it to avoid long wait times. You can book an appointment, arrive at your scheduled time, and complete the process efficiently.

Cost: Free. There is no charge for obtaining your Codice Fiscale.

Step 4: Tessera Sanitaria (Healthcare Card)

Once you're registered in the municipal Anagrafe and issued a Codice Fiscale, you're automatically enrolled in the Italian National Health Service (Servizio Sanitario Nazionale—SSN). This enrollment is automatic and costs you nothing—you're now a covered resident. However, you'll need a physical Tessera Sanitaria (healthcare card) as proof of enrollment and to access services. This card is essential; you must present it whenever you visit a public healthcare provider, fill prescriptions, seek hospital care, or access emergency services.

The Tessera Sanitaria is issued by your local ASL (Azienda Sanitaria Locale—Local Health Authority). Your municipality will provide you with the contact information and address for your ASL, or you can find it through www.salute.gov.it by selecting your region. You can request your card by visiting the ASL office in person with your Codice Fiscale and residency certificate, or many regions now allow online requests through their websites. Processing time is typically 1–2 weeks; urgent cards can be issued same-day in some regions if you explain your circumstances. When you receive your card, verify that your name, Codice Fiscale, and health identification number are all correct—errors can complicate healthcare access.

Your Tessera Sanitaria is a plastic card approximately the size of a credit card, displaying your name, Codice Fiscale, unique health identification number (tessera numero), and validity dates. You must present it whenever you visit a public healthcare provider. It's also used for prescriptions (which you pick up at a pharmacy), hospital admissions and discharges, specialist referrals from your general practitioner, and emergency room visits. Keep it safe but accessible—losing it requires replacement through the ASL, which takes time.

Cost: Free or a very small fee (€5 or less) in some regions. The card itself is free in most regions; some charge a minimal administrative fee for replacement or expedited issuance.

Timeline and Coordination

The ideal sequence, realistic timeline, and critical path is: arrive in Italy → immediately book a Questura appointment for residence permit registration (within 8 days if non-EU) → register with municipal Anagrafe (within 20 days—many do this while waiting for Questura appointment) → once you have Permesso di Soggiorno and Anagrafe registration, obtain your Codice Fiscale (within a few days of Anagrafe registration, as Agenzia delle Entrate is the quickest office) → request your Tessera Sanitaria from the ASL (can be done anytime after Codice Fiscale, processing takes 1–2 weeks). Total time: 4–6 weeks from arrival to having all documents in hand, though many processes overlap and can be parallelized. If the local Questura is backlogged (common in major cities like Rome, Milan, Naples), the Permesso di Soggiorno alone can take 2–3 months—but you can proceed with Anagrafe registration and Codice Fiscale while waiting for the permit. Start immediately upon arrival; don't delay any step.

4. Tax and Fiscal Registration

Once you have your Codice Fiscale, you're officially registered in Italy's tax system. The Italian tax system is comprehensive and strict; depending on your situation—employed, self-employed, retired, student, or combination—different obligations apply. Italy taxes worldwide income for residents (those registered in the Anagrafe are considered tax residents), so you must declare all income, regardless of its source country.

Employed Expats

If you're employed in Italy by an Italian company or employer, your employer handles most tax registration automatically. When you start employment, you'll provide your Codice Fiscale and your employer will register you with INPS (Instituto Nazionale della Previdenza Sociale—National Social Security Institute) for mandatory social security contributions. Your employer then withholds IRPEF (income tax) from your salary according to government withholding tables and remits it to the government on your behalf. This happens automatically; you don't file estimated taxes or quarterly payments.

You'll receive annual payroll and tax documentation (CUD—Certificato Unico Dipendente) each February, showing your gross salary, taxes withheld, social security contributions deducted, and other relevant information. This documentation is essential for your annual tax return (Dichiarazione dei Redditi). Italian income tax is progressive, ranging from 23% to 43% depending on your income level, with significant regional and municipal surcharges adding another 0.4–3.33% depending on your location. Employee social security contributions are approximately 9.19% of gross salary, deducted automatically by the employer, plus employer contributions (around 42% of gross salary) paid by the employer.

You must file a tax return annually if you earned any employment income in Italy. Filing typically opens in May and closes in June each year. Many expats use a commercialista (tax accountant) to prepare and file their returns; expect to pay €300–800 annually for professional accounting and tax services, which is often tax-deductible. Your accountant will review your CUD, confirm your deductions and allowances, prepare your return, and file it electronically with the Agenzia delle Entrate.

Self-Employed and Freelance Expats

If you're self-employed, freelance, or running a business in Italy, you must register as a "Lavoratore Autonomo" (self-employed worker) with the Agenzia delle Entrate. Visit the tax office with your Codice Fiscale, your residence certificate, and a description of your business activity or professional services. The tax office will assign you an economic activity code (codice ATECO) based on your business type, which classifies your business for tax, statistical, and regulatory purposes.

Self-employed workers in Italy must register with INPS for social security contributions, which are substantially higher than employee contributions—typically 25–33% of net income depending on your sector. This is a significant financial obligation and one of the main reasons Italy's self-employment taxation is heavier than employee taxation. You'll pay quarterly estimated taxes (acconti d'imposta) based on your projected annual income, calculated from prior-year returns. At year-end, you file a complete tax return (Dichiarazione dei Redditi) reconciling actual income to estimated payments, plus an income statement (Modello Redditi) showing all revenue and deductible business expenses.

Italy requires electronic invoicing for virtually all business-to-business transactions and many business-to-consumer transactions, managed through the Sistema di Interscambio (SDI—Exchange System). Your invoices must be created in a specific format, transmitted electronically to the SDI, and automatically forwarded to customers. This system is mandatory and enforced—paper invoicing is no longer acceptable for most business purposes. Many accounting software providers (Fatture in Cloud, Voxel, Danea, etc.) handle electronic invoicing for you.

As a self-employed expat, you must establish a business address in Italy—typically your residence, though some coworking spaces can serve this purpose. You cannot operate a business from a foreign address while serving Italian clients. Your business address must be documented in your INPS registration and your tax registration.

Retired Expats and Pension Income

If you're retired and receiving a pension from your home country, you must declare this income to Italian tax authorities and register it with the Agenzia delle Entrate. Pension income is treated as earned income for tax purposes, and you'll be taxed on the full amount if you're an Italian tax resident (which you are once registered in the Anagrafe). You'll file annual tax returns showing your pension income even if your pension is taxed in your home country; Italy doesn't provide automatic exemptions for foreign-source income.

Many retired expats are subject to Italian wealth taxes (Imposta Municipale Immobiliare or IMU) on property owned in Italy, even if not lived in. Additionally, retirees must maintain proof of sufficient income and health insurance as conditions of their residency permit. Keep annual income statements and banking records showing consistent income from your pension. Italy offers special tax regimes for certain retirees and wealthy individuals relocating to Italy, such as the "Decreto Emigrati" (for returning Italians) or special treatment for foreign source income. If you're a returning Italian citizen or meet specific wealth or income thresholds, consult a commercialista or tax attorney to determine if you qualify for preferential treatment, as this can significantly reduce your tax burden.

VAT and Business Expenses

If you're operating a business in Italy with annual turnover exceeding approximately €65,000 (this threshold changes periodically), you must register for VAT (IVA—Imposta sul Valore Aggiunto) at the standard rate of 22% (with higher rates of 5% or 10% for certain goods and services, and lower rates for specific categories). VAT registration is handled through the Agenzia delle Entrate and requires electronic invoicing and monthly or quarterly VAT returns (Dichiarazione IVA). You collect VAT from customers, remit it to the government, and claim credits for VAT you've paid on business expenses. This adds significant administrative complexity, so small businesses try to stay under the VAT threshold when possible.

5. Setting Up a Bank Account

You absolutely need an Italian bank account for employment (salary deposits), paying rent, receiving healthcare reimbursements, and general financial life in Italy. International transfers from abroad are possible but significantly slower and more expensive (typically costing €15–50 per transfer with poor exchange rates) than using a local account. Banks in Italy are increasingly digital, but opening an account still typically requires some in-person verification, especially if you're a foreign national. However, many digital banks now enable online opening with video verification.

What You Need to Open an Account

Most Italian banks require: valid passport (original or certified copy), Codice Fiscale, residency certificate (Certificato di Residenza from the Anagrafe), and proof of income or employment (employment contract, recent payslips, pension statement, or proof of self-employment registration). Some banks additionally ask for proof of initial deposit (often €0–100 minimum), though many offer accounts with no minimum balance. You'll complete a client information form (modulo di adesione or similar), sign anti-money-laundering compliance forms (antiriciclaggio), and authorize electronic communication methods (email, SMS, online banking). Processing typically takes 1–5 business days; many banks now offer online opening with video verification and document submission, though you may need to visit a branch once to activate your card and confirm identity.

Best Banks for Expats

Major traditional Italian banks include UniCredit, IntesaSanPaolo, and Banco BPM, all with extensive branch networks in every Italian city and towns, English-speaking staff in major cities (though not guaranteed in smaller towns), and full-service offerings. However, digital and challenger banks like N26, Revolut, Wise (formerly TransferWise), and BBVA have become increasingly popular with expats and international residents for lower fees, multi-currency accounts, and modern digital interfaces. Opening accounts with these banks online is often easier than traditional banks, though you'll need to provide documentation and may still need video verification. For international transfers, Wise and similar fintech services often offer significantly better rates than traditional banks—Wise's exchange rates are near-interbank rates, and their fees are transparent and low, making them valuable for regular international transfers even if you also maintain a traditional Italian bank account.

Costs and Fees

Most Italian bank accounts charge annual maintenance fees (€0–200 depending on account type and bank), per-transaction fees for ATM withdrawals or bank transfers (€0–3), and fees for foreign currency transactions or international transfers (typically 1–3% of the transaction amount plus a fixed fee). Some "expat-specific" accounts market lower fees than standard resident accounts. Read the terms and conditions carefully; many accounts for residents are cheaper than those marketed as international or expat accounts. Compare at least 3–4 banks before selecting; differences can amount to €100+ annually. Digital banks often have lower fees but may offer fewer services or branch locations if you prefer in-person banking.

6. Healthcare Registration and Coverage

Italy has one of the world's best public healthcare systems, the Servizio Sanitario Nazionale (SSN), consistently ranking among the top 10 globally for quality, accessibility, and efficiency. Once you're registered in the municipal Anagrafe and have your Tessera Sanitaria (healthcare card), you're covered for public healthcare at no point-of-service cost for most services (funded through income taxes and social contributions). This is one of Italy's greatest advantages for residents and expats.

Public Healthcare (SSN)

Public healthcare in Italy is provided through your local ASL (Azienda Sanitaria Locale—Local Health Authority), which is a regional entity managing hospitals, clinics, and health services in your province. Upon enrollment in the SSN, you'll choose a medico di medicina generale (general practitioner—GP) from a list of available doctors in your area. This GP is your first point of contact for any health issues and must refer you to specialists for specialized care. You're entitled to change your GP once per year if unsatisfied. In major cities, many GPs speak English or work in international health centers; in smaller towns, English-speaking doctors may be less common.

Specialist visits and treatments are available free or at low cost-sharing fees (typically €10–50 depending on the service and region) if referred by your GP. Hospital admissions are free for medically necessary care. Prescription medications cost either €0 or a small co-payment (€0–€5) depending on the drug and whether it's on the national formulary. Some services like dental care (except emergencies), vision correction, and physiotherapy are covered only partially or not at all, prompting many expats to also purchase supplementary private insurance to cover these gaps.

EU citizens can use their EHIC (European Health Insurance Card) for emergency and necessary medical treatment throughout the EU, including Italy, at public rates until their card expires. This card is now being replaced by the EDHIC (European Digital Health Insurance Certificate). Request the new card from your home country's health system before moving to Italy. The EHIC remains valid but is being gradually replaced, so request the EDHIC to ensure continuous coverage.

Private Healthcare

Many expats purchase supplementary or primary private health insurance for faster access to specialists (often without GP referral requirements), comprehensive dental coverage, vision correction coverage, and guaranteed English-speaking doctors. Private health insurance in Italy costs €500–3,000+ annually depending on age, health status, and coverage level. Major providers include Allianz, Generali, AXA, and Bupa (which specializes in international coverage). Some employers provide private health insurance as part of employment packages, particularly for international or executive employees. If your employer offers it, evaluate the coverage relative to private plans—employer plans are often comprehensive and subsidized.

Registration Process

Once you have your Tessera Sanitaria, you're automatically part of the SSN, but you must register with your local ASL office or through your chosen GP's office to formally designate your primary care provider. You'll be asked to choose a GP from a list of available doctors in your area, organized by neighborhood. Many GPs maintain their practices in small neighborhood clinics; others are part of larger health centers. Visit your chosen GP's office or the ASL to formalize your selection. After registration, you can schedule appointments directly with your GP or request referrals to specialists through their office.

7. Property and Rental Requirements

Renting Residential Property

Most expats initially rent residential property in Italy rather than purchasing. Italian rental agreements (contratto d'affitto) vary in terms but typically specify the lease duration, monthly rent amount, payment terms and method, conditions regarding maintenance and damage, and tenant obligations. Leases are commonly structured as 4+4 agreements (4-year initial term with automatic 4-year renewal) or as shorter 1–2 year agreements for furnished or seasonal rentals.

Key documents you'll need when renting: your passport and Codice Fiscale (to verify identity), recent employment contract or income documentation, proof of income (payslips, bank statements), professional references if requested, and sometimes a guarantor (a person willing to personally guarantee your rent payment if you default). Landlords often request references from previous landlords or employers, particularly for non-EU citizens. In competitive rental markets like Rome, Milan, and Florence, landlords may request higher security deposits, guarantors, or proof of significantly higher income than the rent itself.

Rental agreements in Italy must be registered with the municipal tax office (Agenzia delle Entrate) within 30 days of signing. Registration costs €50–100 for a standard residential lease. Both landlord and tenant technically bear equal responsibility for registration, though in practice the landlord usually initiates the process with the assistance of a real estate agent or notary. Failure to register the lease is illegal and can result in fines for both parties. Ensure your landlord completes this registration before or immediately after you sign.

Rent control (canone concordato—agreed rent system) exists in Italy for certain residential properties: if both parties agree, you can register at a reduced rate (typically 25–40% below market rent), which provides tax benefits and lower rent, though not all properties qualify. Without this agreement, market-rate rent applies. Discuss this option with your landlord if available for your property.

Security deposits (cauzione) are typically 1–3 months of rent, held in escrow either by the landlord or a third party. The landlord must return the deposit within 30 days of lease termination, minus any legitimate deductions for damage beyond normal wear and tear. Keep all correspondence, lease documents, and inspection photos as protection against wrongful deductions. Document the property's condition with photos before move-in and request a written inspection report from the landlord at the time of signing.

Buying Property

If you plan to buy property in Italy, you'll need a Codice Fiscale and typically an Italian bank account. The property purchase process involves a preliminary contract (compromesso or contratto preliminare), a professional property inspection (perizia), appraisal, title search, and transfer at a notary's office. Transfer taxes are approximately 3–10% of the purchase price depending on your circumstances (foreign buyers pay higher rates in some regions; first-home buyers may qualify for reductions). Notary fees typically run 1–2% of the purchase price. The complete process from offer to closing usually takes 2–4 months. Non-EU citizens face fewer restrictions now than historically, though some regions historically had limitations on foreign ownership of certain properties. Consult a local real estate lawyer about any restrictions in your specific region.

8. Driving Licence Exchange

If you plan to drive in Italy, you need a valid driving licence. EU/EEA licences are recognized in Italy; you can drive on your home country's licence for up to 12 months from establishing residence. After 12 months, you must exchange it for an Italian licence (patente di guida italiana). You don't lose your original licence; you exchange it for an Italian equivalent while your original is returned to your home country's authorities.

Non-EU licences are valid for 12 months from your date of entry into Italy and cannot be exchanged for an Italian licence; you must pass the full Italian driving test (written exam in Italian and practical driving test). The test is conducted entirely in Italian, and most regions do not offer English-language versions. You'll need to study Italian driving rules and the specific test format, often with the help of a driving school that specializes in international students.

To exchange an EU licence for an Italian one, visit your local Motorizzazione Civile (Department of Motor Vehicles), typically located at the Provincial Administration office or as a separate facility in major cities. Bring your original foreign licence, Italian residency certificate (Certificato di Residenza), Codice Fiscale, 4 passport-sized photos (4x4 cm), completed application form (available at the office or online), and payment for the exchange fee. Exchange is usually free or costs €5–15; processing typically takes 1–2 weeks. Your Italian licence is valid for 10 years and must be renewed before expiration.

9. Business and Self-Employment Registration

If you're starting a business in Italy, your registration options and requirements depend on your business structure. Most expats choose between three main structures:

Ditta Individuale (Sole Proprietorship): The simplest and least expensive option. You operate as a self-employed professional with no separate legal entity. Registration (iscrizione) is done with the Agenzia delle Entrate (tax authority) and the Chamber of Commerce (Camera di Commercio) of your province. No separate legal entity is created; you're personally liable for all business debts and obligations. Start-up costs are minimal (€100–300 total for registrations and initial filings). This is suitable for freelancers, consultants, and service providers.

Società a Responsabilità Limitata (SRL—Limited Liability Company): Requires creation of a separate business entity with separate tax liability. Requires a minimum capital contribution (typically €1–25,000, depending on your specific structure), comprehensive Articles of Association (statuto), and formal registration with the Companies Register (Registro Imprese) and notary involvement. This offers liability protection—your personal assets are separate from the company's debts. Costs are higher (€500–2,000+ in notary fees, registration fees, and initial accountant costs) and ongoing compliance is more complex, requiring audited financial statements and annual filings. Ideal for larger ventures, companies with multiple shareholders, or those with significant liability exposure.

Freelancer (Professionista): For regulated professions (lawyers, architects, engineers, accountants, tax consultants, etc.). Requires professional qualifications (degree, certification), registration with the relevant professional order or association, and compliance with professional standards and ethics rules. You're self-employed but subject to professional association rules, continuing education requirements, and ethical codes.

All business registrations require a Codice Fiscale and a documented Italian address for the business (your residence, commercial office space, or coworking address). You'll also register with INPS for self-employed social security contributions (25–33% of net income) and INAIL (insurance against workplace accidents) if required by your sector. For most sectors, INAIL is mandatory. Expect to spend 2–4 weeks on the complete registration process, with costs of €300–2,000+ depending on your structure.
