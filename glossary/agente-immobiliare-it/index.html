Agente Immobiliare

An agente immobiliare is a licensed Italian estate agent who brokers property transactions and is entitled to commission (provvigione) from both buyer and seller upon conclusion of a sale.

Regulation and Licensing

Italian estate agents (agenti immobiliari) must hold a professional licence issued by the local Camera di Commercio (Chamber of Commerce) and register with the national Ruolo degli agenti di affari in mediazione (mediators' register). Unlicensed agents are forbidden from collecting commission — any commission agreement with an unlicensed agent is void and unenforceable. Buyers should always verify the agent's licence before engaging or paying any fee.

Dual Commission: Buyer and Seller

A distinctive and often surprising feature of the Italian property market is that the agent typically collects commission from both buyer and seller — the agent is a mediator (mediatore) between the parties, not exclusively the seller's representative. Commission rates are not legally fixed but are typically 2–3% from each party (plus 22% VAT on the commission amount). On a €400,000 purchase, the buyer may owe the agent €8,000–€12,000 in commission.

When Commission Is Owed

Under Article 1755 of the Italian Civil Code, the agent's commission is earned when a binding agreement (proposta di acquisto accepted by the seller, or the compromesso) is concluded. This means the commission is owed even if the transaction later fails — unless the failure is due to a suspensive condition expressly agreed in the original offer. Buyers should be aware that signing an accepted proposta di acquisto triggers commission liability regardless of whether a rogito ever takes place.

Exclusive vs Non-Exclusive Mandates

Sellers sign an agency mandate (incarico di mediazione) with the agent. Exclusive mandates (mandato in esclusiva) prevent the seller from using other agents or selling privately during the mandate period — typically 6–12 months. Non-exclusive mandates allow multiple agents. Sellers should read the mandate carefully for auto-renewal clauses and termination notice requirements.

MLS and Market Access

Italy has no centralised MLS (Multiple Listing Service) comparable to many Anglo-Saxon markets. Major cities have multiple competing agencies each with proprietary listings — a property may be listed by several agents simultaneously with different prices or information. Buyers covering a market seriously may need to engage multiple agents and cross-reference listings. National portals (Immobiliare.it, Casa.it) aggregate most listings but may lag agent databases by days.

Common Mistake

Watch out: Signing an accepted proposta di acquisto thinking you can still walk away without consequence. Once the seller accepts, commission is owed to the agent regardless of whether the sale completes.

Expert Tip

Pro tip: Ask the agent for their Ruolo registration number before instructing them — verify it on the Camera di Commercio portal. An unregistered agent cannot legally collect commission.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I negotiate the agent's commission?

Yes — especially on higher-value properties. Agents routinely accept 1.5–2% from each party on transactions above €600,000.

Is the buyer's commission tax-deductible?

Agency commission paid by the buyer on a primary residence purchase can be deducted for income tax purposes under specific conditions — confirm with a commercialista.

Do I need an agent to buy in Italy?

No — private sales are legal, but agents control the majority of marketed stock in most Italian cities.
