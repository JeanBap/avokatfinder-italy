Italy Legal Term

Atto di Compravendita

Italian: Atto di Compravendita (Rogito)

The final Italian property deed of sale — the notarised public document that legally transfers ownership from seller to buyer. Also called the rogito notarile. Ownership transfers at the moment of signing.

TL;DR The final Italian property deed of sale — the notarised public document that legally transfers ownership from seller to buyer. Also called the rogito notarile. Ownership transfers at the moment of signing.

The atto di compravendita — universally known as the rogito notarile or simply the rogito — is the notarised deed of sale that formally and irrevocably transfers ownership of Italian property from the seller (venditore) to the buyer (acquirente). It is the climax of the Italian property transaction: a formal ceremony before the notaio at which both parties are present, the document is read aloud in full, and signatures are collected. At the moment of signing, ownership legally passes.

The rogito is drafted by the notaio based on the details in the compromesso and incorporating all additional information gathered during the due diligence period: the property's cadastral data, mortgage search results, planning compliance declaration, tax declarations of both parties, and the agreed financial terms including the payment of any remaining balance. The document is in Italian; foreign buyers without Italian must have a sworn interpreter present.

The financial mechanics at the rogito are precise. The buyer must have the purchase price available — typically transferred by banker's draft (assegno circolare) or bank transfer confirmed before the signing. The notaio deducts any existing mortgages from the sale proceeds and pays them directly to the seller's bank. The remaining net proceeds are then paid to the seller. The notaio simultaneously remits all applicable taxes to the Agenzia delle Entrate.

After signing, the notaio files the rogito with the Conservatoria dei Registri Immobiliari (property register) — typically within a few days. This registration creates the public record of ownership and protects the buyer against any subsequent claims or dispositions by the seller. From the moment of filing, the transfer is effective against the whole world (erga omnes), not just between the parties.

One practical nuance for expats completing remotely via procura (power of attorney): ensure your attorney has a fully up-to-date procura that specifically authorises the rogito on the terms agreed. A procura that pre-dates significant changes to the purchase price or conditions may be insufficient, requiring an updated document — causing delays to completion.

⚠ Common Mistake Arriving at the rogito without confirmed cleared funds. Italian notai will not proceed if the purchase price is not demonstrably available — a bank transfer confirmation or certified banker's draft is required. Wire transfers from overseas can take 2–3 business days; plan accordingly and initiate transfers at least a week before the scheduled rogito date.
💡 Expert Tip Request a draft of the rogito from the notaio at least five working days before signing. Review it carefully with your Italian lawyer or bilingual adviser — checking personal details, property description, cadastral data, price stated, and tax declarations. Errors caught before signing are easily corrected; errors discovered after signing require a costly deed of rectification (atto di rettifica).

Frequently Asked Questions

What happens if either party fails to appear at the rogito?

Failure to appear without a valid power of attorney or prior agreement constitutes breach of the compromesso. The innocent party is entitled to enforce the contract judicially (esecuzione in forma specifica) — Italian courts can order a property transfer even without the seller's consent — or claim damages equal to at least double the caparra paid.

Is the rogito immediately public record?

Yes — once filed at the Conservatoria, the rogito is part of the public property register. Any person can search the register and discover who owns a property. This transparency protects buyers but also means property ownership in Italy is not private.

Can the rogito be cancelled after signing?

Generally no — the rogito is irrevocable. It can only be set aside by a subsequent mutual agreement (atto di mutuo dissenso) or by court order in cases of fraud, duress, or fundamental misrepresentation. Post-completion unwinding is complex, costly, and rarely achievable. Prevention through thorough pre-completion due diligence is the only reliable protection.
