Italy Legal Term

Proposta di Acquisto

Italian: Proposta di Acquisto

A written purchase offer submitted by a buyer to purchase Italian property at a specified price within a set deadline. Once accepted by the seller, it creates a binding preliminary agreement.

TL;DR A written purchase offer submitted by a buyer to purchase Italian property at a specified price within a set deadline. Once accepted by the seller, it creates a binding preliminary agreement.

The proposta di acquisto (purchase proposal) is the first formal step in most Italian property transactions involving estate agents. It is a written document submitted by the buyer to the seller — typically through the agent — setting out the proposed purchase price, the proposed completion date, any conditions (such as mortgage approval), and a deadline by which the seller must accept or reject the offer. The buyer usually accompanies the proposta with a deposit (caparra) of 1–5% of the offered price held by the agent.

This stage is legally significant and often underestimated by foreign buyers. Unlike a simple letter of intent, a proposta di acquisto signed by the buyer and accepted by the seller creates a binding preliminary contract. If the seller accepts, both parties are legally committed to proceed to the compromesso (formal preliminary contract) and then the rogito (final deed) on the agreed terms. Withdrawing without justification at this stage entitles the other party to damages.

The caparra (deposit) paid at the proposta stage is usually a deposit in the nature of a caparra confirmatoria: if the buyer withdraws without legitimate cause, they forfeit it. If the seller withdraws, they must return double the amount. The legal nature of the deposit — whether confirmatoria or penitenziale (a pre-agreed penalty for withdrawal) — should be explicitly stated in the document, as it determines remedies available on breach.

Foreign buyers should exercise particular caution at the proposta stage because it often happens quickly, under pressure from agents eager to secure a commission. Key due diligence on the property's legal status, planning compliance, and mortgage situation has typically not yet been completed at this point. Ensure any proposta includes a condition suspending the obligation pending satisfactory results of such checks.

After the proposta is accepted, the transaction proceeds to the compromesso — a more detailed formal contract typically drafted by a notaio or lawyer. The compromesso repeats and expands the terms of the proposta, adds standard protective clauses (condizioni sospensive), and sets the final completion date. The period between proposta and compromesso is the optimal window for due diligence.

Key Facts: Proposta di Acquisto
Legal effectBinding on both parties once seller accepts
Accompanied byCaparra (deposit) of 1–5% of offered price
Seller deadlineStated in the offer — typically 3–15 days to accept or reject
Default remedyCaparra confirmatoria: buyer loses deposit; seller owes double on withdrawal
Next stepCompromesso (formal preliminary contract) within days or weeks
⚠ Common Mistake Signing and submitting a proposta di acquisto without any suspensive conditions (condizioni sospensive). A proposta with no conditions is a commitment to buy regardless of what due diligence reveals — even if a mortgage is refused or the property has serious planning violations.
💡 Expert Tip Always insert at least two suspensive conditions in your proposta: (1) approval of a mortgage within a stated period if you need financing, and (2) satisfactory results of a legal and technical check (verifica urbanistica e ipotecaria) by your chosen notaio or lawyer within a reasonable timeframe.

Frequently Asked Questions

What happens to my deposit if the seller rejects my proposta?

If the seller simply rejects the offer within the stated deadline, the deposit is returned in full. No binding contract has been formed, and no compensation is owed on either side. The caparra only becomes binding if the seller formally accepts.

Can I negotiate after submitting a proposta?

Once the seller accepts the proposta as written, negotiation is concluded — the terms are fixed. If you want to negotiate further, submit a revised proposta or withdraw the original before acceptance. Partial acceptance by the seller (accepting but at a different price) is legally a counter-offer, not an acceptance.

Is the proposta the same as the compromesso?

No — they are separate documents. The proposta is the offer stage; the compromesso is a fuller preliminary contract usually drafted with notarial involvement that sets out all terms in detail. Both can be binding, but the compromesso is the primary pre-completion commitment and the point at which a larger caparra (typically 10–20%) is paid.
