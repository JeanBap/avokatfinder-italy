Agenzia delle Entrate

Italian: Agenzia delle Entrate

Italy's national tax authority responsible for administering all taxes, issuing codici fiscali, registering property contracts, and handling tax disputes. Every foreigner buying or working in Italy interacts with it.

TL;DR Italy's national tax authority responsible for administering all taxes, issuing codici fiscali, registering property contracts, and handling tax disputes. Every foreigner buying or working in Italy interacts with it.

The Agenzia delle Entrate (AdE) is the Italian Revenue Agency — the government body responsible for the assessment, collection, and enforcement of all Italian taxes. For foreign nationals, it is the unavoidable first point of contact for almost every legal and financial activity in Italy: obtaining a codice fiscale (tax identification number), registering property purchase contracts, filing annual tax returns, and resolving any tax disputes.

The agency operates through a network of regional and provincial offices (uffici territoriali) across Italy, as well as an online portal (agenziaentrate.gov.it) that handles many services digitally. As a foreign national, you can appoint an Italian commercialista (chartered accountant) or avvocato (lawyer) to act on your behalf through a power of attorney, which is strongly advisable if you do not speak Italian.

One of the most critical functions of the Agenzia delle Entrate for property buyers is the registrazione (registration) of preliminary sale contracts (compromessi). In Italy, any private contract for a property worth more than €77,000, or any contract regardless of value if signed before a notaio, must be registered with the AdE within 20 days. Failure to register triggers penalties and can have legal consequences for the enforceability of the contract.

The AdE also administers the Catasto — Italy's land and property registry — and issues visure catastali (land registry extracts) that describe a property's category, class, size, and cadastral value. These values determine certain taxes including IMU (municipal property tax) and imposta di registro (registration tax on property purchases).

For expats receiving income from Italian sources — rental income, freelance work, capital gains from Italian property — an annual Italian tax return (dichiarazione dei redditi) must be filed with the AdE even if you are not tax-resident in Italy. Italy has tax treaties with most major countries to prevent double taxation, but you must actively claim treaty relief — it is not applied automatically.

⚠ Common Mistake Failing to register a preliminary sale contract (compromesso) with the Agenzia delle Entrate within the 20-day deadline. This triggers automatic penalties (sanzioni) of 30% of the registration tax due, plus interest — an avoidable cost that a good commercialista handles routinely.
💡 Expert Tip Register for the AdE online portal with your codice fiscale as soon as you obtain it. Even as a non-resident, the portal lets you check your Italian tax position, download tax certifications, and receive official correspondence electronically — avoiding issues with paper mail to foreign addresses.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need to visit an Agenzia delle Entrate office in person?

Many services are available online or through authorised intermediaries (commercialisti, CAF centres, notai). However, first-time codice fiscale applications for foreign nationals without an Italian residence permit are typically done in person at an ufficio territoriale or Italian consulate abroad.

What is the difference between the Agenzia delle Entrate and the Catasto?

Historically separate, the Catasto (land registry) was merged into the Agenzia delle Entrate in 2012. Today, the AdE manages both tax administration and property cadastral records, which is why it is involved in almost all Italian property transactions.

Can the Agenzia delle Entrate access my foreign bank accounts?

Italy participates in the OECD Common Reporting Standard (CRS) and FATCA. Foreign banks in participating countries automatically report account information to their local tax authority, which shares it with the AdE. Undisclosed foreign income is increasingly difficult to conceal and subject to severe penalties.
