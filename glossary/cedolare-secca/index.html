Cedolare Secca

Italian: Cedolare Secca

A flat-rate Italian tax regime for residential rental income — 21% for standard contracts, 10% for agreed-rent contracts (canone concordato). Replaces IRPEF income tax and registration tax on the lease.

TL;DR A flat-rate Italian tax regime for residential rental income — 21% for standard contracts, 10% for agreed-rent contracts (canone concordato). Replaces IRPEF income tax and registration tax on the lease.

Cedolare secca is an optional flat-rate tax regime available to individual owners of residential property in Italy who rent their property under private rental contracts. Instead of paying progressive IRPEF (personal income tax) on net rental income at rates up to 43%, plus the annual imposta di registro on the lease contract, landlords who opt for cedolare secca pay a single flat rate of 21% on gross rental income — with no deductions allowed.

A reduced rate of 10% applies to contracts using the canone concordato (agreed rent) format — where the rent is negotiated within bands set by local landlord and tenant associations, typically resulting in rents below market levels in exchange for the tax advantage. The canone concordato regime is available in high-density comuni where the municipality has approved the local rent tables.

The election for cedolare secca must be made at the time of registering the rental contract with the Agenzia delle Entrate and must be maintained for the entire duration of the contract (with the option to revoke at annual renewal). Crucially, if a landlord opts for cedolare secca, they cannot raise the rent during the contract term — the rent agreed at the start is frozen until renewal.

For foreign nationals owning Italian rental property, cedolare secca is one of the most practical tax choices — the flat rate is simple to calculate and eliminates the need to integrate rental income into a complex Italian IRPEF return. However, it is only available for residential property let to individuals. Commercial lettings, furnished rentals exceeding a certain scale (which may constitute business income), and lettings by corporate entities are excluded.

Since 2024, cedolare secca applies at 26% for short-term furnished rentals (locazioni brevi — contracts under 30 days) from the second property onwards. The first short-term rental property in your portfolio retains the 21% rate. This change specifically targeted Airbnb-style investors operating multiple properties and has significantly affected the economics of short-term rental portfolios in Italian tourist cities.

⚠ Common Mistake Choosing cedolare secca without checking whether the canone concordato 10% rate is available in your property's comune. In many Italian cities, the 10% rate requires simply using the local agreed-rent format — halving the tax rate with minimal additional bureaucracy.
💡 Expert Tip If you own multiple Italian rental properties, calculate whether the progressive IRPEF regime (with allowable deductions for mortgage interest, maintenance, and depreciation) might produce a lower effective rate than the 21% flat cedolare secca on higher-value portfolios. For most small-scale landlords, cedolare secca wins on simplicity and tax saving.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I switch between cedolare secca and IRPEF each year?

You must maintain your elected regime for the duration of each contract. At renewal or when signing a new contract, you can change your election. You cannot switch mid-contract without revoking the cedolare secca election, which triggers back-payment of avoided registration tax.

Does cedolare secca apply to Airbnb income?

Short-term rentals (under 30 days) can use cedolare secca at 21% for the first property (26% from the second). However, Airbnb and other platforms are required to withhold 21% tax at source on payments to Italian landlords — a mandatory advance tax collection system introduced to tackle undeclared income.

Is cedolare secca available to companies?

No — cedolare secca is available only to individual natural persons (persone fisiche) who own residential property. Companies and other legal entities must declare rental income through their corporate tax returns under standard rules.
