Italy Legal Term

Condominio

Italian: Condominio

An Italian jointly-owned building where individual owners hold private apartments (unità immobiliari) and share ownership of common areas. Governed by the Civil Code and an administrator (amministratore di condominio).

TL;DR An Italian jointly-owned building where individual owners hold private apartments (unità immobiliari) and share ownership of common areas. Governed by the Civil Code and an administrator (amministratore di condominio).

A condominio in Italy is the legal structure for multi-owner buildings — the equivalent of a French copropriété or a UK commonhold. When you purchase an apartment in a condominio, you own your private unit (unità immobiliare) outright and simultaneously hold a proportional share (millesimi) of the common areas (parti comuni): the building structure, roof, stairwells, lifts, garden, and any shared facilities. This dual ownership is automatic and cannot be separated.

Every condominio with more than four units must have a professional administrator (amministratore di condominio) appointed at the annual assembly (assemblea condominiale). The amministratore manages day-to-day operations, maintains accounts, enforces the condominio rules (regolamento condominiale), and handles contracts with service providers. Owners are jointly responsible for the administrator's actions within the scope of their mandate — if the administrator fails to pay the building's insurance, for example, all owners bear the consequences.

The millesimi system determines each owner's voting weight in the assembly and their share of common expenses (spese condominiali). Millesimi are calculated when the building is first divided into condominial ownership and are set out in a tabella millesimale — a document that every buyer should request and review. Owners of larger or better-located units typically hold more millesimi and pay higher spese; owners of ground-floor units may have reduced millesimi for lift costs.

Monthly or quarterly spese condominiali (condominium charges) cover: routine maintenance, cleaning of common areas, electricity for communal lighting, building insurance, the administrator's fee, and contributions to the fondo cassa (reserve fund) for future major works. When purchasing a property, verify that the seller has no outstanding spese arrears — unpaid charges in Italy follow the unit, not the owner, meaning the buyer can inherit liability for the previous owner's debts for the current and preceding year.

For expat buyers, the language barrier in condominio management is a practical challenge. Assembly meetings are conducted in Italian, notices are posted in Italian, and the regolamento condominiale is in Italian. If you cannot attend or participate, you may grant a proxy (delega) to another owner, a lawyer, or even the administrator. Many Italian cities have English-speaking administrators in areas with high expat concentrations.

⚠ Common Mistake Not requesting a dichiarazione liberatoria from the amministratore before completing your purchase. This certificate confirms the seller has no outstanding spese condominiali arrears. Without it, you risk inheriting the previous owner's unpaid charges — potentially thousands of euros for a building with ongoing works.
💡 Expert Tip Before signing the compromesso, ask your notaio to request the verbali delle assemblee (assembly minutes) for the last three years and the condominio's financial statements. Look for any large works already voted but not yet completed — these will become your liability as new owner even if they were voted before your purchase.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I rent out my condominio apartment for short-term holiday lets?

Unless the regolamento condominiale explicitly prohibits it, short-term letting is generally permitted. However, some Italian condomini — particularly in residential buildings with long-term residents — have passed rules restricting or prohibiting tourist rentals. Check the regolamento carefully before purchasing for short-term rental purposes.

What voting majority is required to approve major works in a condominio?

Ordinary maintenance requires a majority of attendees representing at least one-third of millesimi. Extraordinary works require an absolute majority (more than half of all millesimi, not just those present). Structural changes affecting appearance require two-thirds of total millesimi. Innovation (sopraelevazione, new shared facilities) requires four-fifths of millesimi.

Is the amministratore personally liable if they mismanage the condominio funds?

Yes — the amministratore has a fiduciary duty to the condominio and is personally liable for misappropriation of funds, failure to file tax declarations, or improper management. Professional administrators typically carry indemnity insurance. Owners can vote to remove the administrator at any time by simple majority and, if there is evidence of misappropriation, may file a criminal complaint.
