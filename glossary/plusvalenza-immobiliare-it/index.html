Plusvalenza Immobiliare

Plusvalenza immobiliare is Italy's capital gain on property disposal, taxable at 26% (or as ordinary income) when a property is sold within 5 years of purchase, subject to important exemptions.

When Is Plusvalenza Taxable?

Under Italian tax law (Article 67 of the TUIR income tax code), a capital gain (plusvalenza) arising from the sale of immovable property is taxable only when the property is sold within 5 years of its original purchase date. Properties held for more than 5 years are sold free of Italian capital gains tax. This five-year rule is one of the most important planning parameters for property investors in Italy.

Primary Residence Exemption

Even within the five-year holding period, gains on the sale of the seller's primary residence (abitazione principale) are exempt from plusvalenza tax — provided the seller has used the property as their main home for the majority of the ownership period. This exemption applies to the seller (not the buyer) and must be declared in the rogito. The exemption covers most owner-occupier scenarios and makes primary residence sales tax-free regardless of holding period.

Tax Rate Options

For taxable plusvalenze (sale within 5 years, non-primary residence), the seller has two options: (1) include the gain in the ordinary income tax return and pay at marginal rates (up to 43%); or (2) elect for a substitutive flat tax (imposta sostitutiva) of 26%, calculated and paid directly to the notary at the time of the rogito. In practice, the 26% flat tax is almost always chosen as it is simpler and usually results in a lower effective rate for typical sellers.

Calculating the Gain

The gain = net sale price minus acquisition cost minus allowable expenses. The acquisition cost is the price paid at purchase (or, for inherited property, the cadastral value at the date of inheritance). Allowable deductions include notary fees on acquisition, agency commission on acquisition, and documented renovation and improvement costs carried out within the five-year holding period. A geometra or commercialista can optimise the calculation by ensuring all legitimate costs are included.

Non-Resident Sellers

Non-resident sellers are subject to Italian plusvalenza tax on Italian property gains at the same 26% substitutive rate — payable to the Italian state at the rogito. Their home country may also tax the gain (at its domestic rate), with relief under any applicable double tax treaty. The treaty credit mechanism typically ensures no double taxation of the same gain, but timing and procedural requirements vary by treaty — non-resident sellers should seek bilateral tax advice before agreeing the sale timeline.

Common Mistake

Watch out: Selling an investment apartment 4 years and 11 months after purchase, assuming the gain is minor. A one-month delay to cross the 5-year mark eliminates the entire tax bill.

Expert Tip

Pro tip: If you are approaching the 5-year mark, calculate the tax saving vs the carrying cost of waiting — on a €100,000 gain, the 26% tax is €26,000. One extra month of ownership costs very little in comparison.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is the 5-year rule measured from the purchase date or the notarial deed?

From the date of the notarial deed (rogito) — confirm the exact dates with your notary when planning the disposal.

Are renovated properties treated differently?

Generally no, though if the renovation constituted a substantial reconstruction, the 5-year period may restart from the completion of works — seek advice on your specific circumstances.

Does the 26% rate apply to non-residents?

Yes — the same substitutive rate applies, paid to the Italian notary at closing.
