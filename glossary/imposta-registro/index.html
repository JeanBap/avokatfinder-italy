Italy Legal Term

Imposta di Registro

Italian: Imposta di Registro

Italian registration tax levied on property purchases. For second homes and investment properties bought by non-residents, the rate is 9% of the cadastral value. Primary residence buyers pay a reduced 2% rate.

TL;DR Italian registration tax levied on property purchases. For second homes and investment properties bought by non-residents, the rate is 9% of the cadastral value. Primary residence buyers pay a reduced 2% rate.

Imposta di registro is the principal transfer tax on Italian property purchases — the equivalent of stamp duty in the UK or droits de mutation in France. It is calculated on the cadastral value (rendita catastale × a coefficient) rather than the actual sale price for residential purchases between private individuals, which typically results in a significantly lower tax base than the market price. For commercial property or purchases from developers, different rules apply.

The standard rate for individuals purchasing a non-primary-residence (seconda casa) or investment property is 9% of the cadastral base, with a minimum charge of €1,000. For EU citizens and non-EU nationals with a valid Italian residence permit who transfer their residence to Italy within 18 months of purchase, the agevolazione prima casa (first home benefit) applies, reducing the rate to 2%. This 2% rate is one of Italy's most valuable tax reliefs for newcomers — but it comes with strict conditions.

To claim the prima casa benefit, the buyer must: not own another property in the same comune (municipality) already benefiting from the relief, transfer residence to the comune where the property is located within 18 months, and not sell the property within 5 years (or repay the tax benefit plus 30% penalty if they do). Non-EU nationals without an Italian residency permit cannot claim the prima casa rate — a significant cost consideration for non-resident purchasers.

When purchasing from a developer (construction company selling within 5 years of completion), the transaction is subject to IVA (VAT) instead of imposta di registro. The IVA rate is 4% for prima casa properties, 10% for standard residential, and 22% for luxury properties (classified A/1, A/8, A/9). The imposta di registro in these cases is a fixed €200.

In addition to imposta di registro, buyers pay imposta ipotecaria (mortgage registration tax, typically €50 for private sales) and imposta catastale (land registry tax, also typically €50). The notaio collects all three taxes simultaneously at completion and remits them directly to the Agenzia delle Entrate. Total acquisition taxes for a second home purchase are typically 9–10% of cadastral value, or 4–10% of sale price depending on the cadastral ratio.

⚠ Common Mistake Assuming the cadastral value is close to the market price. In popular areas of Italy, the cadastral value can be 30–60% below the actual purchase price, making the imposta di registro calculation much more favourable than buyers from higher-tax countries expect. Always ask your notaio for the estimated tax calculation upfront.
💡 Expert Tip If you plan to move to Italy and use your purchase as a primary residence, commit to transferring your residency (residenza anagrafica) to the property's comune within 18 months. This cuts your imposta di registro from 9% to 2% — potentially saving tens of thousands of euros on a typical Tuscan or Ligurian property purchase.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can non-EU nationals claim the prima casa reduced rate?

Only if they hold a valid Italian residence permit (permesso di soggiorno) at the time of purchase, OR if they commit to applying for residency and transfer their residency to the property's comune within 18 months. Without either condition, the 9% rate applies.

What happens if I claim prima casa and then sell within 5 years?

You must repay the tax saving (difference between 9% and 2% you paid) plus a 30% penalty on the amount reclaimed, plus interest. The only exception is if you purchase another Italian primary residence within one year of the sale.

Is imposta di registro calculated on the actual sale price or the cadastral value?

For residential property sales between private individuals, buyers may elect to be taxed on the cadastral value (rendita catastale × 120 for non-prima-casa) — this is almost always lower than the actual price and is the standard choice. Sales from developers are taxed on the actual sale price via IVA.
