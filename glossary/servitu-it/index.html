Servitù

A servitù (easement) in Italy is a real right that burdens one property (the serving estate) for the benefit of another property or the public, such as a right of way or drainage easement.

What Is a Servitù?

A servitù prediale (property easement) under Articles 1027–1099 of the Italian Civil Code is a real right burdening one piece of land (the fondo servente — serving estate) for the benefit of another piece of land (the fondo dominante — dominant estate). Common types include: servitù di passaggio (right of way), servitù di veduta (right of view), servitù di stillicidio (drainage easement), and utility corridor rights. Servitù attach to the land and bind all successive owners — they do not terminate when a property is sold.

Sources of Servitù

Servitù in Italy arise by: the law (servitù coattive — compulsory easements, e.g. access rights for landlocked parcels); by deed between landowners (servitù volontarie); by usucapion (acquisitive prescription — 20 years of continuous, open, uninterrupted exercise for apparent servitù); or by destination of the owner (destinazione del padre di famiglia — when a single owner divides land in a way that would have required an easement if the two parts had belonged to different people). Each source creates different rules for proof and extinction.

Registration and Third-Party Effect

Contractual servitù must be registered in the Conservatoria dei Registri Immobiliari (Land Registry) to be enforceable against third parties — specifically subsequent purchasers of the serving estate. An unregistered contractual servitù is valid between the original parties but not binding on a buyer of the burdened land who had no notice. Apparent servitù (those with visible signs, such as a physical path) can create third-party notice even without registration, complicating the analysis.

Due Diligence

A thorough Italian property search should include a registry search for registered servitù and a physical inspection of the property for apparent signs of servitù use (worn paths, drainage channels, overhead lines). The visura catastale alone does not show servitù — a full registry extract (visura ipotecaria) is required. For rural properties particularly, undisclosed servitù di passaggio for agricultural access are a common source of post-purchase disputes.

Extinguishing a Servitù

Contractual servitù can be extinguished by: express release (notarial deed, with registration); non-use for 20 years (prescription); merger (the same person becomes owner of both estates); and impossibility of use. A servitù that has become excessively burdensome may be challenged in court, but Italian courts are reluctant to extinguish validly constituted easements without strong justification.

Common Mistake

Watch out: Relying only on the catasto to check for encumbrances. The cadastral database does not record servitù — only a full ipotecaria search at the Conservatoria reveals registered easements.

Expert Tip

Pro tip: For any rural or older urban property, walk the boundaries physically and look for physical signs of third-party use — tyre tracks, drainage channels, or power lines crossing the plot may signal unregistered servitù in use.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does a servitù reduce property value?

A servitù di passaggio across a garden or access route can meaningfully reduce value — factor it into price negotiations.

Can I build over a servitù?

Generally no — works that impede the use of a servitù are unlawful and must be removed at the owner's expense.

What if my neighbour claims a right of way I don't know about?

Demand documentary evidence (registration extract or proof of 20-year prescription) — verbal assertions alone do not establish a servitù.
