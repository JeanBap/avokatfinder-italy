Planimetria Catastale

Italian: Planimetria Catastale

The official floor plan of a property as registered with the Italian Catasto (land registry). Must match the property's actual layout — discrepancies are a common cause of Italian property sale delays and can create legal liability.

TL;DR The official floor plan of a property as registered with the Italian Catasto (land registry). Must match the property's actual layout — discrepancies are a common cause of Italian property sale delays and can create legal liability.

The planimetria catastale is the official cadastral floor plan of a property — a schematic drawing filed with the Catasto showing the layout, dimensions, and room designations of the property as officially registered. Italian law requires that the planimetria filed with the Catasto matches the property's actual physical state at the time of any sale. If the two differ, the sale cannot proceed until the cadastral records are updated to reflect reality.

Discrepancies between the planimetria catastale and the actual layout of a property are extremely common in Italy, particularly in older properties that have been modified, extended, or reconfigured over the decades without the owners updating the cadastral records. Common issues include: walls that have been moved or removed, additional rooms or bathrooms added, mezzanine levels built within existing height, and balconies enclosed to create additional living space.

Before any sale can complete, the notaio is required to obtain a conformità urbanistica e catastale declaration — confirming that the property's actual state matches both the building permits on file at the comune and the planimetria at the Catasto. If neither matches, the seller must either regularise the works (sanatoria) through the appropriate applications or demonstrate they benefit from amnesty (condono edilizio) for historical works.

The cost and time to resolve cadastral discrepancies varies widely. A simple update to a planimetria for a minor internal modification — handled by a geometra (land surveyor) — can cost €500–2,000 and take a few weeks. More serious planning violations (abusi edilizi) that were never regularised require full retrospective planning applications and can take months or even be impossible to resolve, potentially killing the transaction.

For foreign buyers, planimetria checks are a critical part of pre-purchase due diligence. Ask your notaio to obtain a visura catastale and planimetria from the Catasto for the property early in the transaction. Compare the floor plan carefully with what you are actually shown — a room not on the planimetria, or a structural wall in the wrong position, is a warning sign that requires investigation before you commit.

⚠ Common Mistake Assuming that because the property has been sold multiple times previously, the planimetria must be correct. Previous owners and notai may have overlooked discrepancies, particularly minor internal modifications. Each sale requires a fresh verification — do not rely on historical completions as proof of compliance.
💡 Expert Tip Before signing the compromesso, ask your notaio to pull the current planimetria catastale and share it with you. Walk through the property yourself with the floor plan and check every room, wall, and space against the drawing. Discrepancies you identify before signing give you negotiating power — those discovered at the last minute give the seller leverage to delay.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a DOCFA and why does it matter?

DOCFA (Documenti Catasto Fabbricati) is the official IT procedure used by geometri to file new or amended planimetrie with the Catasto. When a seller says "we need to update the planimetria", this means a geometra will prepare and file a DOCFA. The procedure is straightforward for minor changes but requires the geometra to certify accuracy under professional liability.

Can a property be sold with a known planimetria discrepancy?

A sale can proceed if all parties declare in the rogito that the planimetria does not match the actual state — but both seller and buyer must explicitly acknowledge this, and the seller accepts liability for regularising at their own cost after completion. This arrangement is risky for buyers and should be avoided unless a significant price reduction is agreed.

Does the planimetria discrepancy affect my mortgage?

Italian banks typically require a technical report (perizia) from an independent surveyor confirming the property's compliance before granting a mortgage. A known planimetria discrepancy will cause the bank's surveyor to flag the issue, potentially blocking mortgage approval until resolved. Always check cadastral compliance before applying for a mortgage.
